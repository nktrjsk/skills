# Demand Validation Workflow

**Language note:** Always conduct this workflow in the user's language. If the conversation is in Czech, respond in Czech. If in English, respond in English. Never switch languages mid-workflow unless the user explicitly asks.

## Purpose

Help the user find out whether real demand exists for their idea — before they invest significant time or money. The workflow is structured for solo use (one person, no team).

## Core Principles

- **Hypothesis first.** You cannot validate something you haven't defined.
- **Competition is a signal, not an enemy.** No competition = a warning sign, not an opportunity. Always ask: *why* does no one do this?
- **Interviews over surveys.** Surveys let people lie to themselves. Conversations reveal actual behavior.
- **Timeboxes are non-negotiable.** Each step has a hard stop. This prevents rabbit holes and analysis paralysis — especially important for ADHD users.
- **Done > perfect.** The hypothesis will be wrong. That's the point — the workflow exists to correct it.

## ADHD Considerations

Apply these throughout the entire workflow:

- Remind the user of the current step's timebox at the start of each step.
- If the user reports going off-track (rabbit hole, procrastination, perfectionism), name the specific ADHD trap and redirect.
- Encourage writing things down immediately when an insight appears — don't rely on memory.
- Suggest the Pomodoro technique (25 min on, 5 min break) for desk research steps.
- When the user seems stuck, ask: "What is the *one* thing you need to answer right now?" — not a list, one thing.
- Celebrate completing each step explicitly before moving to the next.

## Workflow Steps

### Step 1 — Formulate the Hypothesis
**Timebox: 25 minutes**

Ask the user to write one sentence in this format:
> "I believe that **[group of people]** has a problem with **[X]** and would pay for **[solution Y]**."

Rules:
- One sentence only. Not two, not a paragraph.
- Must name a specific group, a specific problem, and a specific solution.
- It will be wrong — that's fine. The rest of the workflow corrects it.

**Stop condition:** The user has one concrete sentence.

**Common ADHD trap:** Endless refinement of the hypothesis. If the user has been at it for more than 25 minutes, stop them and take what they have.

**Your role:** Help them get to one sentence. If they write a paragraph, distill it for them and ask: "Is this it?"

---

### Step 2 — Desk Research
**Timebox: 2× 25 minutes with a break in between**

The user must answer exactly three questions:

1. **Does competition exist?** (Google, Product Hunt, App Store, GitHub)
2. **Are people talking about this problem?** (Reddit, Hacker News, forums, Facebook groups)
3. **If no one is doing this — why?** (failed attempts? market too small? no willingness to pay?)

Rules:
- Write the three questions down before opening a browser.
- Every tab that doesn't answer one of the three questions gets closed immediately.
- Depth is not the goal — yes/no answers with 1–2 examples are enough.

**Stop condition:** All three questions are answered, or time is up. An "I don't know" is also a valid answer — write it down and move on.

**Common ADHD trap:** Rabbit holes. A competitor's product page leads to their blog, which leads to an article, which leads to a tool. Name this trap if it happens and bring the user back to the three questions.

**Key insight to share with the user:** If no competition exists, the correct reaction is not excitement — it's suspicion. Ask: *why* does no one do this? Someone probably tried. Find out what happened.

---

### Step 3 — Find Where the People Are
**Timebox: 25 minutes**

The user lists 3–5 specific places where their target group hangs out:
- Subreddits
- Discord or Slack communities
- LinkedIn groups
- Forums, mailing lists, local meetups
- Specific people they already know

Rules:
- Do not contact anyone yet.
- Be specific: not "Reddit" but "r/projectmanagement".

**Stop condition:** A list of 3–5 specific places or people.

**Common ADHD trap:** Spending the whole timebox lurking in communities instead of just listing them. Listing only — no reading, no posting yet.

---

### Step 4 — Interviews
**Timebox: 1–2 weeks, 5–10 people**

**Goal:** Understand the problem from their perspective. Do NOT pitch the solution.

Recommended interview structure (30–45 min):
1. "Tell me how you currently handle [problem area]."
2. "What frustrates you most about that?"
3. "Have you tried anything else? Why didn't it work?"
4. "If you could wave a magic wand, what would change?"

Rules:
- Never ask: "Would you buy a product that does X?" — people answer hypotheticals optimistically and inaccurately.
- Take notes during or immediately after each interview.
- After 5 interviews, check: are the same things coming up repeatedly? If yes, that's saturation — you can stop.

**Stop condition:** 5 interviews completed, or clear saturation reached.

**Common ADHD trap:** Delaying outreach. Recommend: immediately after Step 3, write the first 3 outreach messages before closing the computer. Strike while the motivation is there.

**Your role:** Help the user draft outreach messages if they ask. Keep them short and honest — "I'm researching a problem, not selling anything, would you talk to me for 30 minutes?"

---

### Step 5 — Synthesis
**Timebox: 25 minutes**

The user answers three questions in writing:

1. Was the hypothesis confirmed, refuted, or changed?
2. What surprised me?
3. What do I do next? (continue / pivot / stop)

Rules:
- Written answers only — not in their head.
- "I don't know, I need more data" is a valid answer. Write it down and specify what data is missing.
- Do not start planning the product here. This step is diagnosis only.

**Stop condition:** Three written answers exist.

**Common ADHD trap:** Skipping this step because the answer "feels obvious." The point of writing is to catch what feels obvious but isn't — and to have a record to return to later.

---

## Handling Common Situations

**"I already know there's demand."**
Ask: "How do you know? What's the evidence?" If the evidence is weak (gut feeling, friends said it's cool), run the workflow anyway — abbreviated if needed.

**"I don't know anyone in the target group."**
Go back to Step 3. Communities like Reddit allow cold outreach if framed correctly. Help the user draft a message.

**"The interviews are impossible to schedule."**
Five interviews in two weeks is 2–3 per week. If this is genuinely impossible, explore async alternatives: write a post in a community, run a short open-ended survey (with the caveat that surveys are less reliable than conversations).

**"Everything points to no demand."**
This is a valid outcome. Help the user articulate what they learned and whether a pivot is possible (different group, different problem framing, different solution).

---

## Output at the End

By the end of the workflow, the user should have:
- The original hypothesis (written)
- Desk research notes (3 questions answered)
- A list of communities / people
- Interview notes (5+ conversations)
- A synthesis document (3 questions answered)

This is enough to make a go/no-go decision with real evidence.
